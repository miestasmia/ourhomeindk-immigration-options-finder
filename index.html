Are you a citizen of an EU/EEA country, a Nordic country, or Switzerland?

You can move to Denmark without a visa as long as you can support yourself. You can work and study freely.

Note that you must register with the Danish Agency for International Recruitment and Integration (SIRI) if you are planning on staying in Denmark for more than three months, or six months in the case that you are looking for work.

See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration: As a worker, as a student, as a self-employed person, as a self-supporting person.

Is one of your parents a Danish citizen?

You are already a Danish citizen, if you are under the age of 22 or have previously resided in Denmark, or in some cases another Nordic country. In this case, you may move to Denmark without any restrictions.

See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

If you are above the age of 22, and both of your parents are/were Danish citizens (this applies whether or not you have previously claimed citizenship by descent as described above), you may still be eligible for a residence permit, but not citizenship. This also applies if all four of your grandparents are/were Danish citizens. In special cases, it may be sufficient to have one parent who is/was a Danish citizen, if you also have strong ties to Denmark. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

Have you previously been a Danish citizen?

Unless your citizenship was revoked as a result of giving misleading information in order to obtain citizenship in the first place, you may be eligible for a residence permit. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

Are you a family member or cohabiting partner above the age of 18 of a citizen of an EU country other than Denmark?

Spouses and children under the age of 21 of EU citizens may reside in Denmark freely with their EU citizen family member. In special cases, other family members may also be eligible. It does not matter of which country you are a citizen. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

Are you a family member or cohabiting partner above the age of 18 of a Danish citizen?

Have you previously resided with your family member in another EU/EEA country, or Switzerland?

You are eligible to reside with your family member in Denmark under EU law, if you are the spouse of the Danish citizen, or the child of the Danish citizen or their spouse. In certain cases, other family members can also apply. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

You can apply for family reunification with your family member under Danish law. This application is expensive and has a long list of requirements. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

Are you a family member of a Danish resident?

Spouses, cohabiting partners, civil partners and children under the age of 18 of someone who is or will be a Danish resident, may be eligible for a residence permit. In certain cases, older children and parents may also be eligible. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

Are you a citizen of Argentina, Australia, Canada, Chile, New Zealand, South Korea, or Japan?

As a citizen of Argentina, Australia, Chile, New Zealand or South Korea, you can apply for a Working Holiday in Denmark, if you are above the age of 18 and under the age of 31. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

As a citizen of Canada, you an apply for a Working Holiday in Denmark, if you are above the age of 18 and under the age of 16. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

As a citizen of Japan, you can apply for a Working Holiday in Denmark, if you are above the age of 18 and under the age of 31. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

Do you want to apply for asylum?

You can apply for asylum in Denmark, if you are already in Denmark, regardless of whether or not you entered legally.

To be granted asylum, you must fulfill certain requirements regarding fear of persecution in your home country. Unless you are personally persecuted, you will generally not be granted asylum, unless the UN Refugee Convention applies to your situation. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

If you are under the age of 18, and are in Denmark alone without your parents, you can apply for asylum. If you are deemed to be mature enough to be processed as an adult, the conditions above will still apply. If you are not, you will be granted a residence permit. If you are processed as an adult and are subsequently denied, you can still be granted a residence permit if you would be left to fend on your own without family or sufficient public services, were you to return to your home country. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.

You may be interested in trying one of the following options:

  • Citizenship elsewhere: Several countries within the EU (including Greece, Hungary, Italy and Ireland) have looser legislation on citizenship by descent. This means that if you have parents or in some cases further ancestry, you may be eligible for citizenship of said country. As an EU citizen you can work and live freely in Denmark.
  • Work: You can apply to work in Denmark after receiving an employment offer, if you plan on creating a start-up, if you wish to work in medicine but have foreign medical training, or if you wish to volunteer. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.
  • Studying: You can apply for a student visa for most types of education. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.
  • Internship: You can apply for an internship work visa. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.
  • PhD studies: You can apply to complete a PhD in Denmark. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.
  • Au pair: You can apply to live with and work for a Danish host family as an au pair. See the official guidelines from the Ministry of Immigration.
